Digital Divide: Empirical Study of CIUS 2020

As Canada and other major economies consider implementing “digital money” or Central Bank Digital Currencies, understanding how demographic and geographic factors influence public engagement with digital technologies becomes increasingly important. This paper uses data from the 2020 Canadian Internet Use Survey and employs survey-adapted Lasso inference methods to identify individual socio-economic and demographic characteristics determining the digital divide in Canada. We also introduce a score to measure and compare the digital literacy of various segments of Canadian population. Our findings reveal that disparities in the use of e.g. online banking, emailing, and digital payments exist across different demographic and socio-economic groups. In addition, we document the effects of COVID-19 pandemic on internet use in Canada and describe changes in the characteristics of Canadian internet users over the last decade.

Examining the Role of Child Welfare Worker Characteristics in Their Substantiation Decision

The role of child welfare workers is twofold, to promote the safety of children and youth and to address their wellbeing. This provincially legislated mandate requires child welfare workers to make decisions across the child welfare service continuum. After a report of child maltreatment is investigated, workers are required to assess the veracity of the allegation through the substantiation decision and to determine whether the child has been victimized, which may impact on families' future involvement with services. Little is known whether or how individual worker characteristics impact the substantiation decision.

Digital Adoption and Cyber Security: An Analysis of Canadian Businesses

We examine the integration of digital and financial technologies across Canadian enterprises, using data from the Canadian Survey of Digital Technology and Internet Use and the Canadian Survey of Cyber Security and Cybercrime. A Business Digital Usage Score is developed to assess technological efficiency and econometric models are used to analyze firms cybersecurity practices'. The analysis reveals a significant digital divide among Canadian firms and highlights critical risks associated with poor cybersecurity practices, offering insights for enhancing digital adoption and improved cybersecurity in Canadian businesses.
